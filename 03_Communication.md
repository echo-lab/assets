The two values that I seriously care about in communication and collaboration are transparency and promptness. It is tough to be transparent and prompt, and there are many both psychological and social reasons why we sometimes do not want to be so. However, we, as a lab, should try our best to be transparent and prompt in all aspects of our research.

# Slack

[Slack](https://www.slack.com) is the primary way for us to communicate remotely. Just to make our communication more instant, context-aware, and interactive. This may chance but I have not found any better solution.

I would recommend the following (and keep recommending you)

- Install the Slack Desktop application instead of using it as a web browser application.
- Leave it on all the time when you use computers.
- Turn off all the notifications.
- Use the channel more than direct message (unless there needs to be a reason why it needs 1:1 conversation)

Note that Slack is not good for archival. All the messages are going to go away at some point. If you would like to archive the conversation, use something else (Email).

## Using Github Issues

We are going to use Github Issues for keeping track of any bugs/enhacmenet that we want to make in a programming project. Read this article to master the Github Issues function https://guides.github.com/features/issues/ Make sure to create an issue each time you identify a problem or new task that needs to be done. Assign issues to yourself when you start them. You can create issues without assigning them to yourself, but once something is assigned to you, other will avoid doing it, so it's your responsibility!

On the other hand, please always assign the task to yourself to avoid conflicting/overlapping with other's work. That can cost the team important time and effort! This should be done whether or not you plan on solving the issue immediately. This is an important best practice for tracking progress, identifying who has experience with what code, and so on. Plus, it's an industry-standard required practice.

Read the following article for GitHub usage. https://guides.github.com/introduction/flow/

We are going to use "centralized workflow" in using GitHub. Read this to learn more about it: https://www.atlassian.com/git/tutorials/comparing-workflows#gitflow-workflow

# Social Media Bragging

## Should You Promote Your Paper?

Should you promote your paper acceptance? I generally encourage it,
although I do not believe anyone should feel obligated to use social
media. If social media is not for you, that is perfectly fine.

That said, promoting your work can help:

1.  Increase the visibility of both you and your research within
    academia.
2.  Share useful knowledge with researchers who may be interested in
    your work.

Research consists of both **production** and **distribution**. Writing
papers is only half of the job; the other half is ensuring that the
knowledge reaches the community. Sharing your work before the conference
is one effective way to distribute it.

**Caveat:** Social media also has potential downsides, including
distraction, fear of missing out (FOMO), and impostor syndrome.

## General Advice

-   Focus on the **paper** rather than yourself. Emphasize the problem,
    insights, and contributions instead of treating the post as a
    personal achievement announcement.
-   Wait until you can share the **preprint PDF** so interested readers
    can immediately access the paper.
-   Do **not** ask an LLM to generate the entire post from your paper.
    Proofreading is fine, but writing the post yourself is valuable
    practice in communicating your research.
-   *(Opinionated)* I prefer opening with the research problem rather
    than "I'm excited to share that my CHI paper was accepted."
    Introduce the acceptance later after capturing readers' interest.
-   Another option is to begin with "I have a CHI paper accepted, which
    is about..." This may attract more attention, although I am not sure
    whether it is ultimately more effective.
-   Consider including a short video or a strong visual (e.g., system
    figure, key results, infographic).
-   Tailor your strategy to your career stage:
    -   Early-stage Ph.D. students often benefit from promoting their
        own work.
    -   Later-stage Ph.D. students may benefit more from building a
        recognizable research identity.
-   I will eventually share every paper. If I forget, or if you would
    rather handle the post yourself, let me know.

### Possible Posting Strategies

1.  Student posts first; I repost with comments. *(Preferred for senior
    students.)*
2.  I post first; the student reposts with comments. *(Preferred for
    junior students.)*
3.  I post alone. *(For students who are not interested in social
    media.)*
4.  I do not post because I am not the corresponding author, although I
    sometimes make exceptions if the first author is my student and is
    inactive on the platforms I use.

### Additional Tips

-   Acknowledge your co-authors and tag them if appropriate.
-   Increase visibility by mentioning conference names, co-author
    handles, departments, universities, and relevant keywords.
-   Update your personal website before posting since readers may visit
    it.
-   *(Optional)* Avoid posting during peak acceptance-announcement
    periods. Waiting until the initial flood subsides may help your work
    stand out.
-   *(Optional)* Be aware that social media algorithms may delay when
    posts appear in others' feeds. Promote presentations with sufficient
    lead time.
