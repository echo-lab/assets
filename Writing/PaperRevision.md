## Advice for Writing Response Letters

-   Ideally, write a response letter that makes reviewers feel
    sufficiently reassured that they do not feel the need to check the
    revised paper.
-   Group similar comments into themes and label each reviewer (e.g.,
    **R1**, **R2**, **2AC**).
-   Do not assume reviewers remember their original comments. Briefly
    summarize each concern.
-   Clearly summarize how you addressed each comment.
-   Even when a review seems unfair or based on a misunderstanding,
    assume there was something in the paper that could have been
    communicated more clearly. Improve the paper accordingly.
-   Specify exactly where changes were made (e.g., line numbers,
    subsection numbers, page numbers).
-   In Part 2 of the revision document, briefly summarize what was
    changed and where those changes appear.
-   *(Optional)* Assign a unique identifier (e.g., **R2-1**) to each
    response item and leave the identifier as a manuscript comment so
    co-authors can trace the purpose of each revision.
-   Every revision should be goal-oriented and directly address a
    reviewer comment. General manuscript improvements do not need to be
    highlighted.

### From the Reviewers' Perspective

Reviewers primarily want to:

-   Recall what they previously commented on.
-   Understand, at a high level, how the authors responded.
-   Easily locate the corresponding changes in the manuscript and verify
    them.

Reviewers generally do **not** care about:

-   Every highlighted change in the manuscript.
-   Reading other reviewers' comments or responses.
